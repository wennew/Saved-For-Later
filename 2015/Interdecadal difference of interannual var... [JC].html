The correlation between sea surface temperature (SST) and El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) persists into post-ENSO September over the South China Sea (SCS), the longest of the world ocean. Slow modulations of this correlation are analyzed by using the International Comprehensive Ocean-Atmosphere Data Set (ICOADS). ENSO’s influence on SCS SST experiences significant interdecadal changes for the past 138 years (1870-2007), with a double-peak structure correlation after the 1960s compared to a single-peak before the 1940s. According to the ENSO correlation character, we divide the analysis period into four epochs. In epoch 3 of 1960-1983, the SST warming and enhanced precipitation over the southeastern tropical Indian Ocean, rather than the Indian Ocean basin wide warming, induce easterly wind anomalies and warms up the SCS in the summer following El Niño. Besides the Indian Ocean effect, during epochs 2 (1930-1940) and 4 (1984-2007), the Pacific-Japan (PJ) pattern of atmospheric circulation anomalies helps sustain the SCS SST warming through JJA(1) with easterly wind anomalies. The associated increase in shortwave radiation and decrease in upward latent heat flux persist the SCS SST warming into the summer. Meanwhile, the rainfall response around the SCS to ENSO shows interdecadal variability, strong after the 1980s. The results suggest that both the remote forcing from the tropical Indian Ocean and the PJ pattern are important for the ENSO teleconnection to the SCS and its interdecadal modulations.
