Contribution of Synoptic Transients to the Potential Predictability of PNA Circulation Anomalies: El Niño vs La Niña

JC by Muhammad Adnan Abid et al, 2015-07-02

Contribution of Synoptic Transients to the Potential Predictability of PNA Circulation Anomalies: El Niño vs La Niña

Muhammad Adnan Abid,1 In-Sik Kang,1,2 Mansour Almazroui,1 and Fred Kucharski1,3

1 CENTER of EXCELLENCE FOR CLIMATE CHANGE RESEARCH/DEPARTMENT OF METEOROLOGY, KING ABDULAZIZ UNIVERSITY, JEDDAH, SAUDI ARABIA.

2 SCHOOL of EARTH AND ENVIRONMENTAL SCIENCES, SEOUL NATIONAL UNIVERSITY, SEOUL, KOREA.

3 EARTH SYSTEM PHYSICS SECTION, INTERNATIONAL CENTRE FOR THEORETICAL PHYSICS, TRIESTE, ITALY.

The potential predictability (PP) of seasonal-mean 200-hPa geopotential height (Z200) anomalies in the Pacific and North American (PNA) region is examined for El Niño and La Niña separately by using 50 ensemble members of 20th century AGCM simulations. Observed sea surface temperature (SST) is prescribed for the period 1870-2009, and 14 El Niño and La Niña years after 1900 are selected for the present study. The domain averaged value of PP for Z200 in the PNA region, as measured by the signal-to-noise ratio, for El Niño is about 60% larger than that of La Niña. Such a large PP is mainly due to larger signal and due partly to smaller noise during El Niño compared to La Niña counterparts. The transient eddy feedback to the PNA circulation anomalies is stronger during El Niño events (about 50%) than the La Niña counterpart, and this difference in the transients contributes significantly to the different Z200 signals in the PNA region. The noise variance of the transients during El Niño is about 17% smaller than the La Niña counterpart, and thus transients play an important role in the reduction of Z200 noise during El Niño. Idealized experiments with same spatial pattern but different signs of SST anomalies confirm the results mentioned above. Moreover, these experiments with several different amplitudes of positive and negative phases of tropical Pacific SST anomalies show that signals of Z200 and transients are proportional to precipitation anomalies in the tropical Pacific and noises of Z200 for El Niño cases are somewhat smaller than the corresponding values of La Niña.
