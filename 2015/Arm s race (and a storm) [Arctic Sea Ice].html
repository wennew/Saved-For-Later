Arm's race (and a storm)

Arctic Sea Ice by Neven, 2015-08-28

As I write this, a storm is battering the ice pack on the Pacific side of the Arctic. It's not as huge as the Great Arctic Cyclone of 2012, but it's pretty decent as far as cyclones go, and it's doing its thing in that part of the Arctic where the ice pack looks weakest, but was strongest at the start of the melting season.The timing of this event is perfect for discussing one of the most important aspects of this melting season.

The image on the top right shows the ice age distribution at the start of the melting season, red being the oldest multi-year ice at that time. Due to a very intense high pressure area over the Beaufort Sea at the end of the freezing season, heavy winds drove the ice pack apart there (a so-called cracking event), causing this multi-year ice to become interspersed with very thin ice. That's the dark blue spots between all the red.

During the very first phase of the melting season the Beaufort Sea region witnessed a heat wave causing a lot of this thin ice to melt out. Because the ice pack wasn't pushed back together again, many of the multi-year ice floes were surrounded by warming waters, and ever larger holes within the ice pack on the Pacific side of the Arctic started to form. The animation below shows what happened between week 27 and week 32, or June 29th to August 9th:

Just towards the end of the animation the holes are showing up. Unfortunately these maps aren't updated until the minimum has hit, but we can still get an idea of what happened after August 9th by checking out the sea ice concentration maps provided by the University of Bremen. The following animation shows how the holes separate a so-called 'arm' of multi-year ice from the rest of the ice pack:

Yet again we see a large collection of ice floes detaching themselves from the ice pack (there's also a small patch in the Laptev Sea hugging the coast if you look carefully), and in this case it's that sliver of red we saw on the ice age distribution map, indicating it's 5+ years old. This arm of older, thicker ice was kind of holding on, but is now definitely being finished off by the storm mentioned in the introduction.

So, how about that storm? After coming from the Pacific through Bering Strait it arrived in the Beaufort Sea yesterday where it is staying put now at 985 hPa, after having bottomed out at 983 hPa (according to Environment Canada):

Again, it can't be compared to GAC-2012, which was much bigger and bottomed out at 963 hPa, but can be compared to a storm that battered the Beaufort Sea at the end of August 2011 (around which time I introduced the term 'flash melting', when large patches of sea ice disappear from one day to the next on the Uni Bremen sea ice concentration map).

I've turned the land masses grey to make it easier to determine the cyclone's position, and I've also added a red dot. That's where Barrow, Alaska, is, and it is currently being hit hard by waves. Westerly winds were gusting up to 50 miles an hour, pushing waves up to the top of the beach and causing some erosion, the National Weather Service said.

Fall is a stormy season, and the timing -- right around the annual minimum sea ice extent -- allows the surf to build and reach shore, he said.

Conclusion

In the Winter Analysis I posted back in May I speculated about whether this broad zone of older, thicker ice would protect the ice pack on the Pacific side of the Arctic, like happened in 2010 and 2011. The answer is: it didn't, it got obliterated. The Beaufort and Chukchi Seas proved to be yet again the multi-year ice graveyard instead of the transit they used to be when older ice would make a round-trip around the Arctic and end up even older and thicker against the shores of Greenland and the Canadian Arctic Archipelago.

We have to wait and see what the repercussions of all this will be for sea ice volume, ice age distribution and coming melting seasons, but it's clear that Arctic sea ice isn't in any way recovering.
